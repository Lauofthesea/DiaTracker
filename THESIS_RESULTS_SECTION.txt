5.1 Dataset Overview
--------------------

The DiaTracker system was trained using the NHANES 2021-2023 dataset obtained from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). This dataset contains comprehensive health and dietary information from a nationally representative sample of the United States population.

TOTAL PARTICIPANTS: 2,660 individuals
DATA SOURCE: CDC NHANES 2021-2023
COLLECTION PERIOD: 2021-2023
GEOGRAPHIC COVERAGE: United States (nationally representative)

Dataset Characteristics:
- Age range: 18-80 years
- Gender distribution: 52.3% Female, 47.7% Male
- BMI range: 16.5-48.2 kg/m²
- Fasting glucose range: 70-300 mg/dL


5.2 Data Splitting Strategy
----------------------------

The dataset was divided into three subsets following standard machine learning practices to ensure unbiased model evaluation.

TRAINING SET: 70% of data
- RF Model 1 (Glucose Predictor): 1,675 participants
- RF Model 2 (Risk Classifier): 1,820 participants
- Purpose: Train the Random Forest models to learn patterns

VALIDATION SET: 15% of data (used during cross-validation)
- Integrated into 5-fold cross-validation process
- Purpose: Tune hyperparameters and prevent overfitting

TEST SET: 15% of data
- RF Model 1 (Glucose Predictor): 419 participants
- RF Model 2 (Risk Classifier): 456 participants
- Purpose: Final evaluation on completely unseen data

The test set was held out during the entire training process and only used once for final performance evaluation, ensuring unbiased accuracy estimates.

5.3.2 Target Variable: Simulated Glucose

IMPORTANT NOTE: Since NHANES does not include 1-hour post-meal glucose measurements, the target variable was simulated using a clinically validated formula.

SIMULATION FORMULA:
glucose_1hr = fasting_glucose + (available_carbs × 0.25) + random_noise

Where:
- fasting_glucose: Measured fasting blood glucose (mg/dL)
- available_carbs: Total carbohydrates minus fiber (grams)
- 0.25: Clinical coefficient (10g carbs raises glucose by 2.5 mg/dL)
- random_noise: Normal distribution with mean=0, standard deviation=10 mg/dL

FORMULA SOURCE:
Jenkins DJ, et al. (1981). Glycemic index of foods: a physiological basis for carbohydrate exchange. American Journal of Clinical Nutrition, 34(3):362-366.

Wolever TM, et al. (1991). The glycemic index: methodology and clinical implications. American Journal of Clinical Nutrition, 54(5):846-854.

SIMULATED GLUCOSE STATISTICS:
- Mean: 160.3 mg/dL
- Median: 153.0 mg/dL
- Minimum: 81.0 mg/dL
- Maximum: 300.0 mg/dL
- Standard deviation: 37.8 mg/dL

The simulated values fall within clinically realistic ranges for post-meal glucose levels.

5.3.4 Performance Metrics on Test Set

The model was evaluated on 419 unseen participants from the test set.

REGRESSION METRICS:

ROOT MEAN SQUARED ERROR (RMSE): 11.31 mg/dL
Formula: RMSE = sqrt((1/n) × Σ(predicted - actual)²)
Interpretation: On average, predictions deviate from actual values by 11.31 mg/dL

MEAN ABSOLUTE ERROR (MAE): 8.81 mg/dL
Formula: MAE = (1/n) × Σ|predicted - actual|
Interpretation: Average absolute difference between predicted and actual glucose

R-SQUARED (R²): 0.9109 (91.09%)
Formula: R² = 1 - (SS_residual / SS_total)
Interpretation: The model explains 91.09% of variance in glucose levels

CLINICAL INTERPRETATION:
An RMSE of 11.31 mg/dL is clinically acceptable. For context:
- Continuous glucose monitors have typical RMSE of 10-15 mg/dL
- Clinical decision thresholds are typically 20-30 mg/dL apart
- The model's error is well within acceptable clinical margins

5.6 Glucose Decay Model
------------------------

PURPOSE: Calculate current glucose level at any time point after eating

FORMULA: Current Glucose = Baseline + (Peak - Baseline) × e^(-time/tau)

Where:
- Current Glucose: Glucose level at current time (mg/dL)
- Baseline: Fasting glucose before eating (mg/dL)
- Peak: Maximum glucose 1 hour after eating (mg/dL)
- time: Hours since eating
- tau: Time constant (hours), depends on BMI
- e: Euler's number (2.71828)

TIME CONSTANT (TAU) BY BMI:
- BMI less than 25: tau = 1.5 hours (fast clearance)
- BMI 25-30: tau = 1.8 hours (moderate clearance)
- BMI greater than 30: tau = 2.2 hours (slow clearance)

CITATION:
Wolever TM, et al. (1991). The glycemic index: methodology and clinical implications. American Journal of Clinical Nutrition, 54(5):846-854.

EXAMPLE CALCULATION:
Baseline glucose: 95 mg/dL
Peak glucose: 171 mg/dL
BMI: 28 (tau = 1.8 hours)
Time: 2 hours after eating

Step 1: Elevation = 171 - 95 = 76 mg/dL
Step 2: Decay factor = e^(-2/1.8) = 0.33
Step 3: Current elevation = 76 × 0.33 = 25 mg/dL
Step 4: Current glucose = 95 + 25 = 120 mg/dL

5.8 Limitations and Considerations
-----------------------------------

LIMITATION 1: Simulated Target Variable
RF Model 1 uses simulated glucose_1hr values because NHANES does not include post-meal glucose measurements. While the simulation is based on clinically validated formulas (Jenkins 1981), real-world validation with continuous glucose monitor data is needed.

LIMITATION 2: Dataset Diversity
NHANES 2021-2023 represents the US population. Generalization to other countries or ethnic groups requires validation with additional datasets.

LIMITATION 3: Sample Size
2,660 participants is moderate for medical machine learning. Larger datasets (10,000+) would improve model robustness.

LIMITATION 4: Class Imbalance
High Risk participants represent only 4.22% of the dataset. While this reflects real-world prevalence, it limits the model's exposure to high-risk cases during training.
