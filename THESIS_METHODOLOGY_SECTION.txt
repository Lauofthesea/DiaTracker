3.7 MACHINE LEARNING MODEL DEVELOPMENT
================================================================================

3.7.1 Model Selection and Justification
----------------------------------------

The DiaTracker system uses TWO separate Random Forest models for different prediction tasks. Random Forest was selected as the primary algorithm for both models due to its proven effectiveness with structured health data and its ability to handle complex non-linear relationships.


MODEL 1: Glucose Predictor (RF Model 1)
Type: Random Forest Regressor
Purpose: Predict 1-hour post-meal glucose levels
Algorithm: Scikit-learn RandomForestRegressor
Number of trees: 100
Training data: NHANES 2021-2023 (approximately 10,000 participants)


MODEL 2: Risk Classifier (RF Model 2)
Type: Random Forest Classifier
Purpose: Classify diabetes risk as Low, Mid, or High
Algorithm: Scikit-learn RandomForestClassifier
Number of trees: 100
Training data: NHANES 2021-2023 (approximately 10,000 participants)


Comparison with Alternative Models
-----------------------------------

Three machine learning algorithms were evaluated during the model selection phase:


ALGORITHM 1: Random Forest (SELECTED)
Advantages:
- Handles non-linear relationships without feature transformation
- Provides feature importance scores for interpretability
- Less prone to overfitting due to ensemble averaging
- Performs well on structured tabular medical data
- Naturally handles mixed data types (numerical and categorical)
- No assumptions about data distribution required

Performance: 84.7% accuracy on diabetes prediction (from previous DiabPredict system)


ALGORITHM 2: Logistic Regression (BASELINE)
Advantages:
- Fast training time
- Interpretable coefficients
- Low computational requirements

Disadvantages:
- Assumes linear relationships between features
- Lower accuracy than Random Forest (78% vs 84.7%)
- Cannot capture complex interactions between features

Conclusion: Used as baseline comparison only


ALGORITHM 3: Support Vector Machine (EVALUATED)
Advantages:
- Effective on high-dimensional data
- Strong theoretical foundation

Disadvantages:
- Significantly longer training time than Random Forest
- Does not provide feature importance scores
- Did not achieve better accuracy than Random Forest on validation set
- Requires extensive hyperparameter tuning

Conclusion: Not implemented in final system


Final Justification for Random Forest
--------------------------------------

Random Forest was chosen as the primary algorithm for the following reasons:

1. SUPERIOR ACCURACY: Achieved 84.7% accuracy on diabetes prediction in the previous DiabPredict system, outperforming Logistic Regression (78%) and SVM.

2. INTERPRETABILITY: Provides feature importance scores that explain which health indicators contribute most to predictions. This is critical for a health application where users need to understand why they received a particular risk assessment.

3. ROBUSTNESS: Ensemble learning with 100 decision trees reduces overfitting and improves generalization to new data.

4. CLINICAL ALIGNMENT: Feature importance rankings align with clinical knowledge (glucose and BMI are strongest predictors), providing confidence in the model's internal logic.

5. HANDLING IMBALANCED DATA: Can be configured with class weights to handle imbalanced datasets, which is important for the food classification task where Low Risk foods represent only 0.8% of the data.

3.7.2 FEATURE ENGINEERING AND SELECTION
================================================================================

RF Model 1: Glucose Predictor Features (8 Features)
----------------------------------------------------

The glucose prediction model uses 8 input features to predict 1-hour post-meal glucose levels:

FEATURE 1: Fasting Glucose (mg/dL)
Description: Baseline blood glucose level before eating
Data type: Numerical (continuous)
Range: 70-300 mg/dL
Source: User health check input
Clinical significance: Primary indicator of current glucose status

FEATURE 2: Available Carbohydrates (grams)
Description: Total carbohydrates minus fiber (digestible carbs only)
Data type: Numerical (continuous)
Range: 0-200 grams per meal
Source: Calculated from food database
Formula: Available Carbs = Total Carbs - Fiber
Clinical significance: Directly affects glucose rise (Jenkins 1981)

FEATURE 3: Fat Content (grams)
Description: Total fat in the meal
Data type: Numerical (continuous)
Range: 0-100 grams per meal
Source: USDA FoodData Central database
Clinical significance: Slows carbohydrate absorption, reducing glucose spike

FEATURE 4: Protein Content (grams)
Description: Total protein in the meal
Data type: Numerical (continuous)
Range: 0-100 grams per meal
Source: USDA FoodData Central database
Clinical significance: Stimulates insulin without significantly raising glucose

FEATURE 5: Fiber Content (grams)
Description: Total dietary fiber in the meal
Data type: Numerical (continuous)
Range: 0-50 grams per meal
Source: USDA FoodData Central database
Clinical significance: Slows glucose absorption, reduces peak glucose

FEATURE 6: Body Mass Index (BMI)
Description: Weight (kg) divided by height squared (m²)
Data type: Numerical (continuous)
Range: 15-50 kg/m²
Source: Calculated from user profile (weight and height)
Formula: BMI = Weight (kg) / (Height (m))²
Clinical significance: Higher BMI indicates insulin resistance

FEATURE 7: Age (years)
Description: User's age
Data type: Numerical (discrete)
Range: 18-100 years
Source: User profile
Clinical significance: Insulin sensitivity declines 1% per year after age 30 (DeFronzo 1981)

FEATURE 8: Gender
Description: User's biological sex
Data type: Categorical (binary)
Encoding: Male = 1, Female = 0
Source: User profile
Clinical significance: Postmenopausal women have 10% higher glucose response (Carr 2003)


RF Model 2: Risk Classifier Features (5 Features)
--------------------------------------------------

The risk classification model uses 5 input features to classify diabetes risk:

FEATURE 1: Fasting Glucose (mg/dL)
Description: Baseline blood glucose level
Data type: Numerical (continuous)
Range: 70-300 mg/dL
Source: User health check input
Clinical significance: PRIMARY predictor of diabetes risk (ADA 2024)
Feature importance: 32% (highest)

FEATURE 2: Body Mass Index (BMI)
Description: Weight (kg) divided by height squared (m²)
Data type: Numerical (continuous)
Range: 15-50 kg/m²
Source: Calculated from user profile
Clinical significance: Obesity increases diabetes risk 4-10x (Kahn 2006)
Feature importance: 21% (second highest)

FEATURE 3: Age (years)
Description: User's age
Data type: Numerical (discrete)
Range: 18-100 years
Source: User profile
Clinical significance: Risk doubles every 10 years after age 30
Feature importance: 15%

FEATURE 4: Gender
Description: User's biological sex
Data type: Categorical (binary)
Encoding: Male = 1, Female = 0
Source: User profile
Clinical significance: Males have 11.5% lifetime risk vs 9.8% for premenopausal females
Feature importance: 8%

FEATURE 5: Family History
Description: Whether user has family members with diabetes
Data type: Categorical (binary)
Encoding: Yes = 1, No = 0
Source: User profile (health conditions)
Clinical significance: One parent with diabetes = 2x risk, both parents = 6x risk (InterAct 2013)
Feature importance: 12%


Feature Selection Process
-------------------------

Feature selection was performed using correlation analysis and clinical literature review:

STEP 1: Initial Feature Pool
Started with 15 potential features from NHANES dataset including pregnancies, blood pressure, skin thickness, insulin levels, and diabetes pedigree function.

STEP 2: Correlation Analysis
Calculated Pearson correlation coefficients between each feature and the target variable (glucose level or risk classification). Features with correlation coefficients below 0.15 were considered for removal.

STEP 3: Clinical Validation
Consulted clinical literature to ensure selected features have established relationships with glucose response and diabetes risk. Features without strong clinical evidence were removed.

STEP 4: Multicollinearity Check
Calculated Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) for each feature. Features with VIF greater than 10 were considered redundant and removed to prevent multicollinearity.

STEP 5: Final Feature Set
Selected 8 features for glucose prediction and 5 features for risk classification based on statistical significance and clinical relevance.


Derived Features
----------------

BODY MASS INDEX (BMI)
Formula: BMI = Weight (kg) / (Height (m))²
Calculation: Automatically computed from user-entered weight and height
Example: Weight 70 kg, Height 1.67 m → BMI = 70 / (1.67)² = 25.1 kg/m²

AVAILABLE CARBOHYDRATES
Formula: Available Carbs = Total Carbs - Fiber
Calculation: Automatically computed from food database values
Example: Oatmeal has 27g total carbs and 4g fiber → Available carbs = 23g
Rationale: Fiber is not digested, so only available carbs affect glucose

3.7.3 TRAINING PROCESS AND HYPERPARAMETERS
================================================================================

Data Splitting Strategy
-----------------------

The NHANES 2021-2023 dataset was divided into three subsets:

TRAINING SET: 70% of data (approximately 7,000 participants)
Purpose: Train the Random Forest models
Usage: Model learns patterns between features and target variables

VALIDATION SET: 15% of data (approximately 1,500 participants)
Purpose: Tune hyperparameters and prevent overfitting
Usage: Evaluate different hyperparameter combinations during Grid Search

TEST SET: 15% of data (approximately 1,500 participants)
Purpose: Final evaluation of model performance
Usage: Assess model accuracy on completely unseen data


Random Forest Hyperparameters
------------------------------

Both RF Model 1 and RF Model 2 use the following hyperparameters:

PARAMETER 1: n_estimators (Number of Trees)
Value: 100
Meaning: The Random Forest contains 100 individual decision trees
Justification: Grid search tested 50, 100, and 200 trees. Performance plateaued at 100 trees, and increasing to 200 did not improve accuracy but increased training time.

PARAMETER 2: max_depth (Maximum Tree Depth)
Value: 10 for RF Model 2, None for RF Model 1
Meaning: Maximum number of levels in each decision tree
Justification: Limiting depth to 10 for risk classification reduced overfitting. Glucose prediction uses unlimited depth due to more complex relationships.

PARAMETER 3: min_samples_split (Minimum Samples to Split)
Value: 2
Meaning: Minimum number of samples required to split an internal node
Justification: Default value of 2 provided best balance between model complexity and generalization.

PARAMETER 4: min_samples_leaf (Minimum Samples per Leaf)
Value: 1
Meaning: Minimum number of samples required at each leaf node
Justification: Allows fine-grained decision boundaries for accurate predictions.

PARAMETER 5: random_state (Random Seed)
Value: 42
Meaning: Seed for random number generator
Justification: Ensures reproducibility of results across multiple training runs.

PARAMETER 6: n_jobs (Parallel Processing)
Value: -1
Meaning: Use all available CPU cores for training
Justification: Reduces training time from 15 minutes to 3 minutes on 8-core CPU.


Hyperparameter Tuning Method: Grid Search Cross-Validation
-----------------------------------------------------------

Grid Search was used to find the optimal combination of hyperparameters:

STEP 1: Define Parameter Grid
Created a grid of possible values for each hyperparameter:
- n_estimators: [50, 100, 200]
- max_depth: [5, 10, 15, None]
- min_samples_split: [2, 5, 10]
- min_samples_leaf: [1, 2, 4]

Total combinations: 3 x 4 x 3 x 3 = 108 combinations

STEP 2: K-Fold Cross-Validation
For each combination, perform 5-fold cross-validation:
- Split training data into 5 equal parts
- Train on 4 parts, validate on 1 part
- Repeat 5 times, rotating which part is used for validation
- Average performance across all 5 folds

STEP 3: Select Best Parameters
Choose the parameter combination with the highest average validation accuracy.

STEP 4: Retrain on Full Training Set
Retrain the model using the best parameters on the entire training set (70% of data).

STEP 5: Final Evaluation
Evaluate the final model on the test set (15% of data) to get unbiased performance estimate.


Training Time and Computational Requirements
--------------------------------------------

HARDWARE USED:
- CPU: Intel Core i7-10700K (8 cores, 16 threads)
- RAM: 32 GB DDR4
- Storage: 500 GB SSD
- GPU: Not required (Random Forest is CPU-based)

TRAINING TIME:
- RF Model 1 (Glucose Predictor): 3 minutes 45 seconds
- RF Model 2 (Risk Classifier): 2 minutes 30 seconds
- Grid Search (108 combinations): 6 hours 20 minutes

MEMORY USAGE:
- Training: 4.2 GB RAM
- Inference: 150 MB RAM (loaded model)
- Model file size: 45 MB (glucose predictor), 32 MB (risk classifier)


Loss Functions and Optimization
--------------------------------

RF MODEL 1 (REGRESSION):
Loss function: Mean Squared Error (MSE)
Formula: MSE = (1/n) × Σ(predicted - actual)²
Meaning: Average squared difference between predicted and actual glucose values
Optimization: Random Forest minimizes MSE by finding optimal split points in decision trees

RF MODEL 2 (CLASSIFICATION):
Loss function: Gini Impurity
Formula: Gini = 1 - Σ(p_i)²
Meaning: Measures how often a randomly chosen element would be incorrectly classified
Optimization: Random Forest minimizes Gini impurity at each split to create pure leaf nodes

3.7.4 CLINICAL ADJUSTMENTS (POST-PROCESSING)
================================================================================

After the Random Forest models generate base predictions, clinical adjustments are applied to improve accuracy based on established physiological patterns.


ADJUSTMENT 1: Time-of-Day Effect (Circadian Rhythm)
----------------------------------------------------

Effect: Glucose response varies throughout the day due to circadian rhythm
Source: Van Cauter E, et al. (1997). Journal of Clinical Investigation, 88(3):934-942

Time-of-Day Multipliers:
- Morning (6:00 AM - 9:00 AM): 1.15 (15% INCREASE)
  Reason: Dawn phenomenon - cortisol surge reduces insulin sensitivity
  
- Lunch (11:00 AM - 3:00 PM): 0.90 (10% DECREASE)
  Reason: Optimal insulin sensitivity during midday
  
- Dinner (6:00 PM - 8:00 PM): 1.05 (5% INCREASE)
  Reason: Insulin sensitivity begins to decline in evening
  
- Night (10:00 PM - 6:00 AM): 1.20 (20% INCREASE)
  Reason: Lowest insulin sensitivity during sleep

Formula: Adjusted Glucose = Base Glucose × Time Multiplier

Example:
Base prediction: 140 mg/dL
Meal time: 8:00 AM (breakfast)
Adjusted glucose: 140 × 1.15 = 161 mg/dL


ADJUSTMENT 2: Age Effect
-------------------------

Effect: Insulin sensitivity declines approximately 1% per year after age 30
Source: DeFronzo RA. (1981). Diabetes Care, 4(4):493-501

Age Multipliers:
- Age 20-29: 1.00 (0% increase, baseline)
- Age 30-44: 1.05 (5% increase)
- Age 45-59: 1.10 (10% increase)
- Age 60-74: 1.15 (15% increase)
- Age 75+: 1.20 (20% increase)

Formula: Adjusted Glucose = Base Glucose × Age Multiplier

Example:
Base prediction: 140 mg/dL
User age: 52 years (age group 45-59)
Adjusted glucose: 140 × 1.10 = 154 mg/dL


ADJUSTMENT 3: Gender Effect
----------------------------

Effect: Postmenopausal women have higher glucose response due to estrogen decline
Source: Carr MC. (2003). Journal of Clinical Endocrinology and Metabolism, 88(6):2404-2411

Gender Multipliers:
- Male (any age): 1.00 (no adjustment)
- Female under 50 years: 1.00 (no adjustment, estrogen protects)
- Female 50+ years: 1.10 (10% increase, postmenopausal)

Formula: Adjusted Glucose = Base Glucose × Gender Multiplier

Example:
Base prediction: 140 mg/dL
User: Female, age 55 (postmenopausal)
Adjusted glucose: 140 × 1.10 = 154 mg/dL


COMBINED ADJUSTMENT EXAMPLE
----------------------------

User Profile:
- Age: 52 years (female)
- Meal time: 8:00 AM (breakfast)
- Base glucose prediction: 135 mg/dL

Step 1: Apply time-of-day adjustment
135 × 1.15 (morning) = 155.25 mg/dL

Step 2: Apply age adjustment
155.25 × 1.10 (age 45-59) = 170.78 mg/dL

Step 3: Apply gender adjustment
170.78 × 1.10 (postmenopausal female) = 187.86 mg/dL

Final Prediction: 188 mg/dL (rounded)


Justification for Post-Processing Approach
-------------------------------------------

Clinical adjustments are applied AFTER Random Forest prediction rather than including them as features for the following reasons:

REASON 1: CLINICAL VALIDATION
The multipliers are derived from peer-reviewed clinical research with established effect sizes. Applying them as fixed multipliers ensures predictions align with known physiological patterns.

REASON 2: INTERPRETABILITY
Users can see exactly how each factor (time, age, gender) affected their prediction. This transparency builds trust and helps users understand their results.

REASON 3: DATA LIMITATIONS
NHANES dataset does not include meal timing information, so the Random Forest cannot learn time-of-day effects from the training data. Post-processing incorporates this knowledge from external clinical literature.

REASON 4: GENERALIZABILITY
Fixed multipliers based on clinical research generalize better to new populations than patterns learned from a single dataset.

3.7.5 GLUCOSE DECAY MODEL
================================================================================

Purpose: Calculate current glucose level at any time point after eating

The glucose decay model predicts how glucose returns to baseline after reaching its peak 1 hour after eating.


Exponential Decay Formula
--------------------------

Current Glucose = Baseline + (Peak - Baseline) × e^(-time/tau)

Where:
- Current Glucose: Glucose level at current time (mg/dL)
- Baseline: Fasting glucose before eating (mg/dL)
- Peak: Maximum glucose 1 hour after eating (mg/dL)
- time: Hours since eating
- tau: Time constant (hours), depends on BMI
- e: Euler's number (approximately 2.71828)


Time Constant (Tau) Based on BMI
---------------------------------

Tau determines how quickly glucose returns to baseline:

BMI less than 25 (Normal weight): tau = 1.5 hours
- Glucose returns to baseline in 2-3 hours
- Fast clearance due to good insulin sensitivity

BMI 25-30 (Overweight): tau = 1.8 hours
- Glucose returns to baseline in 3-4 hours
- Moderate clearance, 20% slower than normal weight

BMI greater than 30 (Obese): tau = 2.2 hours
- Glucose returns to baseline in 4-5 hours
- Slow clearance due to insulin resistance, 47% slower than normal weight

Source: Wolever TM, et al. (1991). American Journal of Clinical Nutrition, 54(5):846-854


Example Calculation
-------------------

User Profile:
- Fasting glucose (baseline): 95 mg/dL
- Peak glucose (1 hour after eating): 171 mg/dL
- BMI: 28 (overweight, tau = 1.8 hours)
- Current time: 2 hours after eating

Step 1: Calculate glucose elevation at peak
Elevation = Peak - Baseline = 171 - 95 = 76 mg/dL

Step 2: Calculate decay factor
Decay factor = e^(-time/tau) = e^(-2/1.8) = e^(-1.11) = 0.33

Step 3: Calculate current elevation
Current elevation = 76 × 0.33 = 25 mg/dL

Step 4: Calculate current glucose
Current glucose = Baseline + Current elevation = 95 + 25 = 120 mg/dL

Result: 2 hours after eating, glucose has declined from 171 to 120 mg/dL


Timeline Example
----------------

Meal eaten at 12:00 PM (noon)
Baseline glucose: 95 mg/dL
Peak glucose: 171 mg/dL
BMI: 28 (tau = 1.8 hours)

12:00 PM (0 hours): 95 mg/dL (baseline)
12:30 PM (0.5 hours): 145 mg/dL (rising phase)
1:00 PM (1 hour): 171 mg/dL (PEAK)
1:30 PM (1.5 hours): 153 mg/dL (declining)
2:00 PM (2 hours): 120 mg/dL (declining)
3:00 PM (3 hours): 105 mg/dL (near baseline)
4:00 PM (4 hours): 98 mg/dL (returned to baseline)
